What’s Card Explorer all about, then?
This utility (written, incidentally, a long time before CardEm was released) will allow you to send commands to any ISO card (VideoCrypt, EuroCrypt, GSM phone cards etc.) and monitor the response. Non-standard packet lengths, instruction codes, five-byte headers etc. can be used, the computer can be switched to receive half-way through transmitting a packet and so on. Three types of input are allowed - simple XPL files, Season-style log files or Explorer EasyScript files. EasyScript is a miniature scripting language designed to make probing into a smart card easier. It contains only essential commands at the moment, but we hope to extend it to include more powerful operations in the future.

Can I use it to hack Sky or clone my phone card?
Yes you can, but only if you know the right packets to send to the cards, to make them do what you want, and that is the point of Explorer! In itself, Explorer will not give you any free channels or phone calls, but you can use it to build up your knowledge of the behaviour of any ISO card. Knowledge, which you can do with as you please. Explorer is just one general-purpose tool out of many things you will find helpful in order to crack today’s sophisticated smartcards.

DSS users - how to make your DSS cards work with Explorer

Explorer talks to cards at a speed of 9600 by default. DSS cards operate at a higher speed than this, so you’ll need to add the switch “b38400” when you start Explorer at the command line. Test that everything is going ok with this script:

48 2A 00 00 77
R01 R77 R02

Save it as “test.xpl” and type:
Explorer itest.xpl b38400

This packet should dump the card’s channel tier and PPV information (you will see seven or eight lines of hex digits) if it works correctly. If there is a problem, you will get a timeout error.

Information about NC access cards

Many of you will have heard of the Network Computer, or NC, a set top box which is designed to connect your TV simply to the Internet. The first NCs appearing in the UK have smartcards included, which are currently just used to transfer user name and password data to your ISP when you logon, but will supposedly later be used for electronic shopping and funds transfer, etc.

We obtained one of these cards (though without an NC, so predicting the packet order is very difficult), and here are our first findings:

The baud rate is absolutely standard - 9600 baud for both ATR and data

NC Card ATR: 3f 3f 11 00 05 03 02 97 01 13 00 00 01 a0 00 00 00 00

Class code of card: 0xA0

The card does appear to adhere to the standards for SW1/SW2 and procedure bytes.
