SKIPJACK Declassified

Skipjack is nice if you need a strong cipher in a microcontroller which doesn't have much memory. A software implementation on an 8 bit processor is simple and needs only a tiny amount of RAM, plus 256 bytes of ROM for the F table. The key setup time is zero. It's more complicated then TEA, but it doesn't require 32-bit shifts which are a pain on the 8051.

It's reasonably efficient, compact, easy to implement on 8-bit processors, and needs only 3 or 4 bytes of RAM besides the data block (the key would normally be in EEPROM). I don't know of any other published ciphers that have gotten any serious cryptanalytic attention that are this good for small microcontrollers. The one that comes closest is the GOST block cipher, which requires 32-bit rotations, 256 bit keys, has various other types of bogosity. Skipjack looks better than DES in this situation in just about every way: it's faster, needs less ram and code space, and is more secure because of its longer keys.

The usefull information about SkipJack include:

  • SkipJack declassification press release.
  • NIST page with SkipJack description.
  • Preliminary "Observations on the SkipJack Encryption Algorithm" by Eli Biham and others.
There are some implementations of SkipJack available. We present here C++ implementation, ADA implementation, and Java implementation. When implementing SkipJack by itself you will need some SkipJack test vectors. If you are successful in implementing SkipJack in other environments, please let us know.

  • Issue 3, News of April 15, 1998
    • The new group called Smartcard Developer Association was founded. They chacterized themselves:
      "The Smartcard Developer Association was founded when experts in the field of security software realized that there was little non-proprietary information about smartcards available. Many software developers wish to simply use a smartcard, rather than create their own. Smartcard development tools are expensive and vendor specific. Cross-platform diagnostic and development software is virtually non-existent. This situation is especially difficult for developers wishing to use existing smartcard technology rather than develop their own solutions. The SDA seeks to remedy this situation."
    • GSM SIM Cracked
      The ISAAC security research group and the Smartcard Developer Association announced a flaw in the authentication codes found in digital GSM cellphones. This allows an attacker with physical access to a target phone to make an exact duplicate (a ``clone'') and to make fraudulent calls billed to the target user's account.
      More detailed information about the attack is located here. The "secret" algorithm for GSM authentication and key establishment A3/A8 or COMP128 is now published on the internet.
    • "Magazine articles like to describe cryptography products in terms of algorithms and key length. Algorithms make good sound bites: they can be explained in a few words and they're easy to compare with one another. "128-bit keys mean good security." "Triple-DES means good security." "40-bit keys mean weak security." "2048-bit RSA is better than 1024-bit RSA." But reality isn't that simple."
      It says Bruce Schneier in his interesting article Security Pitfalls in Cryptography.
    • Barclays Bank in England warned shoppers to be on guard against credit-card fraud. Although losses have been dropping, theft using cloned (counterfeit) cards are large enough at 23.6M pounds (about U$40M) to warrant investment in smart cards. In the UK alone, said a Barclays' spokesman, over half a million retail points of sale will have to be converted to interact with the microchips on the new credit cards.
      Source: PA News
    • The European Union floated a proposal for a smart card that would double as a bank card and as a universal identity document. Some anti-EU British politicians immediately branded the proposal as a threat to British sovereignty and a threat to civil liberties.
      Source: PA News 1/21/97
    • The National Criminal Intelligence Service of the UK publicly stated that the growth in electronic commerce and specifically the coming widespread use of electronic money (e.g., the smart card systems of Mondex) will open an enormous vulnerability in the economy. Digital counterfeiting would likely be impossible to distinguish, said Gareth MacLachlan, head of the Organised Crime Unit of the NCIS; he emphasized the value of integrating full audit trails into all forms of electronic money, including electronic "cash" system that are designed at present to provide anonymous financial transactions. Dr James Backhouse of Computer Security Research Centre at the London School of Economics agreed, saying, "The biggest impact would be the loss of any audit trail. Most bank transactions leave some trail within the system and this would be lost."
      Source: Guardian Weekly, Reuter 6/4/97
    • On May 1st 1998, will be launched the world’s largest organized attack against EuroCrypt pay-TV channels, in an attempt to break the encryption used on a number of film, sport and general entertainment packages. The group called "Keyblitz" put their Prelaunch FAQ on the WEB. (Note: if this page is not accessible, try newer browser).
    • An article from Chaos Communication Congress '97 Hacking Chipcards (in German).
    • Differential fault analysis (DFA) is a powerful attack on crypto systems embodied in devices such as smart cards. If the device can be made to deliver erroneous output under stress (heat, vibration, pressure, radiation, whatever) then a cryptanalyst comparing correct & erroneous outputs has a dangerous entry point. An article by Sandy Harris Defending against DFA presents some ways to prevent it.

    • Police Utilise SIM Technology
      The mobile criminal fraternity are being pursued by the ever increasing sophistication of police technology. Suspects caught with digital mobile phone in hand are unwittingly providing the police with a large amount of information which maybe used in trials to verify contacts, times and locations.
      Police in London started the initiative with the purchase of several ORGA Dr Sim devices for the checking and downloading of GSM / DCS SIM Smart Cards and mobile phone data.
      ORGA says that Dr SIM is now approved and recommended to all UK police forces. Developed four years ago, Dr SIM allows non-technical personnel to thoroughly evaluate SIM cards and provide instant customer service at the point of sale.
      (Smart Card News, January 1998)
